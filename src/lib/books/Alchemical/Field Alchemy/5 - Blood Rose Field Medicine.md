# Field Alchemy: Blood Rose Field Medicine
> **Series:** Field Alchemy, Volume 5  
> **Author:** Malcolm Maxworthy, First Head of Chemical Studies  
> **Written:** AD 37
## How I Developed This Formula
Blood rose's regenerative properties were well-documented by Verena's botanical work, but translating plant properties into practical healing formula required extensive testing. I needed something field medics could prepare quickly using minimal equipment—complex preparations fail when you're treating bleeding soldiers.

The breakthrough came from realizing blood rose doesn't require processing beyond grinding. The plant's healing compounds remain stable in simple water suspension. No heating, no special solvents, no complicated technique. Just grind and mix.

This simplicity proved challenging to accept. I spent months trying elaborate extraction methods before concluding the straightforward approach worked best. Sometimes the simple answer actually is the right one, though admitting that continues to bother me.

The waterskin preparation makes this ideal for field use. Soldiers can carry multiple waterskins, medics can prepare batches rapidly, and the formula works reliably without laboratory equipment. Practical healing over theoretical perfection.
## The Formula
**Ingredients:**
- 32 measures blood rose (fresh petals preferred, dried acceptable)
- 1 empty waterskin

**Preparation:**
Grind blood rose petals thoroughly. The deep red petals should become fine crimson powder. Blood rose processes more easily than most plants—the soft petals break down readily.

Transfer ground material directly to waterskin. Add clean water to fill. Shake vigorously for one minute. Let steep minimum 20 minutes. The brew turns deep red and maintains faint sweet scent characteristic of blood roses. Strain before use, though some sediment will remain.

**Effect:**
One waterskin provides significant healing over several seconds. The healing effect works best out of combat as it seems feeling pain of any kind interrupts the regeneration process. This is emergency healing, not combat enhancement.

The effect feels like warm tingling spreading from consumption point outward as tissues repair themselves. Wounds close, bruises fade, broken bones begin knitting. Not instant, but noticeably fast healing.
## Practical Uses
Field medicine represents primary application. Combat medics use this for treating wounded soldiers who need immediate stabilization before proper medical attention. The rapid healing often means difference between survivable injury and fatality.

Emergency situations requiring quick recovery benefit from portable healing. Adventurers carry multiple waterskins for handling unexpected injuries during exploration.

Post-combat recovery allows fighters to return to functionality faster. The healing accelerates natural recovery processes, reducing downtime between engagements.

Some use it preventatively before dangerous activities, keeping waterskins readily available for immediate treatment if injury occurs. Quick access to healing can prevent minor wounds from becoming serious problems.
## Field Testing Results
Military adoption of this formula provided extensive real-world data:

**Effectiveness:** Most test subjects reported significant healing within moments of consumption. Some showed slower response, typically due to severe wounds requiring more than single dose.

**Combat Limitations:** Healing interrupts when taking damage. In active combat, the regeneration stops, wasting the brew. Use requires either breaking contact with enemy or consuming during brief pauses in fighting.

**Multiple Doses:** Sequential waterskins provide cumulative healing until wounds fully close. No diminishing returns observed until complete recovery achieved.

**Storage Duration:** Prepared brew maintains potency approximately one week when properly sealed. Longer storage reduces effectiveness noticeably.
## Development Notes
**Attempt #14:** Thought heating blood rose extract would concentrate healing properties. Wrong. Heat destroyed active compounds, creating inert liquid with no healing effect. Room temperature processing only.

**Attempt #21:** Tried combining blood rose with other healing plants expecting synergistic enhancement. Most combinations provided no improvement over blood rose alone. Some combinations actually reduced healing effectiveness.

**Attempt #28:** Experimented with fermentation thinking aged preparation might increase potency. Result: disgusting taste, no healing enhancement, waste of materials.

**Attempt #35:** Tested whether adding blood essence would create stronger healing. It did not. Just made expensive brew that healed identically to basic formula.

The simple 32:1 water ratio proved optimal through extensive testing. Don't complicate what works.
## Important Warnings
The combat limitation is serious. Don't drink this while actively fighting—the healing will interrupt and you'll waste the brew. Create opening, consume quickly, then reengage.

Multiple rapid doses can cause nausea. Your body processes healing compounds at finite rate. Drinking three waterskins in quick succession doesn't triple healing speed, just makes you sick. Space doses appropriately.

Blood rose brew doesn't prevent damage, only repairs it after occurrence. Don't assume healing availability makes defensive preparation unnecessary. Better to avoid injury than rely on emergency healing.

Store prepared brew away from extreme temperatures. Heat degrades healing compounds rapidly. Cold doesn't destroy them but makes pouring difficult. Keep at moderate temperature for optimal results.
