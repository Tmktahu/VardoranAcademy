# History of the Gilded Compass: The Founding
> **Series:** History of the Gilded Compass, Volume 1  
> **Author:** Ismet Drantiel  
> **Written:** 7 AD

The death of Dracula marked the end of an age, but not the end of conflict. In the vacuum left behind by his fall, the world did not find peace. It found uncertainty. Old powers scrambled to redefine themselves, new ideologies took root, and ancient enmities hardened rather than healed. It was during this unstable period that the organization now known as the Gilded Compass first came into being.

The Gilded Compass did not begin as an independent faction. It emerged instead as an outcast sect of the Church of Luminance, expelled rather than born. The Church, emboldened by its role in opposing Dracula and empowered by its holy doctrines, had grown increasingly rigid in both belief and practice. Its teachings were absolute: vampirism was corruption, coexistence was heresy, and purity could only be achieved through eradication. For many, these convictions brought comfort. For others, they became cause for concern.

Among those who questioned Luminance's path were two individuals whose names would become inseparable from the Compass' earliest history: Solarus and Amadeus. At the time, both were devoted members of the Church. Solarus was already a figure of rising prominence—charismatic, resolute, and unwavering in his faith. Amadeus, by contrast, was a soldier rather than a preacher: disciplined, observant, and increasingly disillusioned by what he witnessed in the Church's campaigns.

Historical accounts indicate that Solarus and Amadeus were once close allies. They fought together, strategized together, and shared a belief that the world required guidance after Dracula's fall. Where they diverged was not in their desire for order, but in their understanding of how that order should be achieved.

Solarus believed in purification. To him, vampirism was an irredeemable stain, and compromise only delayed the inevitable. Amadeus believed in utility and balance. He had seen vampires who governed their hunger, who protected settlements, who contributed meaningfully to the survival of others. He had also seen humans commit atrocities under the banner of holy light. From these observations, Amadeus reached a conclusion that Luminance could not tolerate: that morality was not dictated by blood, but by action.

This belief placed Amadeus at odds with the Church's doctrine. Initially, these disagreements were contained within private councils and closed-door debates. Over time, however, they grew public. Amadeus began to gather like-minded individuals—humans and vampires alike—who shared his conviction that cooperation was not only possible, but necessary. These early assemblies were informal and unrecognized, but they were not unnoticed.

The Church of Luminance responded decisively. The group was declared heretical. Its members were stripped of rank, exiled, or executed, depending on circumstance. Solarus, now firmly entrenched as a leading authority within the Church, did not intervene. Whether this was due to ideological conviction or political necessity remains unclear. What is certain is that from that moment forward, Solarus and Amadeus stood on opposite sides of an irreparable divide.

It was following this expulsion that the Gilded Compass formally took shape.

The name itself was deliberate. A compass does not judge the terrain it traverses; it merely points the way. Gold, though valued, tarnishes less than other metals, yet still bears the marks of age and use. Together, the symbol reflected the faction's central philosophy: direction over dogma, purpose over purity. The Gilded Compass would not promise righteousness, only resolve.

From its inception, the faction adopted principles that sharply contrasted with those of Luminance. Cooperation between humans and vampires was not only permitted but encouraged. However, this tolerance was not extended unconditionally. The Compass did not—and does not—suffer arrogance lightly. Vampires who allowed their immortality or strength to inflate their egos, who sought dominion rather than coexistence, were treated as threats rather than allies. Many were hunted down by the faction itself, their removal framed not as punishment, but as necessity.

Humans, meanwhile, were not welcomed by default. Fear and prejudice were common, and not without cause. Those who joined the Compass were required to demonstrate courage, adaptability, and the capacity to work alongside creatures long taught to be monsters. Weak resolve was considered a liability. Sentimentality, a danger.

In the Gilded Compass, strength was not defined by species. It was measured through loyalty, capability, and contribution. Those who could not uphold these standards were not retained.

To sustain itself, the faction required structure. Early on, it became evident that ideology alone would not suffice. Survival demanded organization, specialization, and accountability. Thus, the Compass divided its operations into three primary sects: Research, Trade, and Security.

The Research Sect was tasked with the accumulation and preservation of knowledge. Its members studied arcane phenomena, alchemical processes, historical texts, and later, the growing instability of magic itself. Their purpose was not curiosity for its own sake, but understanding as a tool for survival and progress.

The Trade Sect oversaw resources, craftsmanship, and exchange. From weapons and armor to alchemical reagents and rare materials, this sect ensured the faction remained supplied and solvent. It also handled external dealings, negotiating with settlements and factions that might otherwise refuse contact with a mixed human-vampire organization. Pragmatism, rather than honesty, guided many of these exchanges.

The Security Sect encompassed both martial and medical roles. Its fighters defended Compass holdings and enforced internal discipline, while its healers treated wounds regardless of origin. Mercy was secondary to effectiveness. Preservation of the faction took precedence over individual grievances.

Membership in a sect was not mandatory. However, those who contributed consistently were afforded greater influence and recognition. This system discouraged idleness and centralized authority around function rather than lineage or charisma.

For fifteen years following its founding, the Gilded Compass remained largely nomadic. Its members moved cautiously, avoiding direct confrontation with Luminance while quietly expanding their numbers. During this period, Amadeus continued to serve as the faction's unifying figure—not through titles or ceremony, but through decisive action and personal involvement.

That period ended abruptly.

Fifteen years after the Compass' formation, Amadeus led a small contingent toward Vardoran. Records suggest that his intent was to confront Luminance directly and, if possible, dismantle Solarus' growing influence. What transpired during that journey remains unknown. Amadeus and his warriors were never seen again. No bodies were recovered. No messages returned.

His death was assumed, though never confirmed.

The loss of its founder did not destroy the Gilded Compass. If anything, it hardened it. Leadership became more distributed. Decision-making slowed but grew more deliberate. The faction survived, not because of a single figure, but because its ideology had already taken root.

This, then, is the beginning of the Gilded Compass: not as a rebellion for rebellion's sake, nor as a sanctuary for the desperate, but as a calculated response to a world that refused to accommodate complexity. It was born of fracture, sustained by discipline, and guided not by light or shadow, but by direction.
